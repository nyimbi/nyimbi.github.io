--- layout: post title: 'Gravitational Waves: What am I missing?' date: '2009-04-30T10:10:00.000+01:00' author: nyimbi tags: - gravitational waves - LIGO - Laser Interferometry modified_time: '2009-04-30T11:22:50.362+01:00' blogger_id: tag:blogger.com,1999:blog-5550057272911021788.post-1150913893795978093 blogger_orig_url: http://nyimbi.blogspot.com/2009/04/gravitational-waves-what-am-i-missing.html --- I recently saw a documentary on the Laser Interferometry Gravitational Wave Observatory
(LIGO), and it was particularly entertaining. It is a subject I haven't thought of for a really long time and it was great seeing that new thinking.

Very simply, the LIGO is essentially two Orthogonal laser beams intersecting at a point and canceling out (interfering). If one of the arms changes in length, because of a gravitational wave, then there will be reduced interference at the point where they intersect - by detecting the change in light intensity/shift etc you can determine the amount by which the length of the arm changed (unless of course the change in length is exactly one wavelength of the light - I suppose). This is an extremely simplistic description, but may cover the main points.

Now consider this:
  1. The speed of light (c) is constant. What that means to me, and this is the crux of the matter, is that in all circumstances a proximal observer will find that the distance traversed by a beam of in a finite fixed time is the same. Simplistically the observer uses a meter rule and measures out the distance traversed in a fixed finite time.
  2. A gravitational wave stretches space-time. (We make a host of assumptions right here.)
  3. Objects properly included in a space stretch or contract with the space. So the observer's meter rule stretches and contracts in the space.
So we now find that:
  1. If c is constant, then if the space stretches of contracts then the wavelength must contract or expand based on the change in the relative contraction of space-time. (We assume that the frequency is fixed - for no real reason actually, we could work with frequency instead of wavelength but I can think of no reason at present to vary frequency)
  2. There is no way for a person in a space to determine that the space he occupies has stretched or contracted, because all means of determining that have altered in just such a manner as to prevent that discovery.
Now on to LIGO:
  1. The premise of LIGO is that the two arms will stretch differently in the presence of a gravitational wave.
  2. The Gedanken experiment we conducted previously, would indicate that no characteristic of light (=laser) will change in any direction because of such a contraction of space time.
  3. At the point of intersection we have a homgeneous space time (assuming a mathematical point - hence homogeneous by definition).
  4. Ergo: There will be no interference. It would appear to me that there can be no detectable interference, so we can't expect anything from this rig.
Now:
I must be missing something essential because a whole bunch of really smart people have bought into this whole business and I can't for the life of me think what it is.
